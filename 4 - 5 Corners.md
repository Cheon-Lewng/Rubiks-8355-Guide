## STEP 4 – LAST CORNERS

### 🎯 Big Goal
Help the last **five corners** finish their journey.  
End with a happy, complete cube.

This step is a **celebration dance**.

---

### 📖 The Story (no moves)
You made it to the end.  
Now we meet the **Fish**.

Fish are corner friends.  
They only swim here, at the very end.

The **Working Door** is still with us.  
This time, it **does not travel**.  
It stays by **your right hand**, the whole time.

The Fish know a dance.  
The dance looks the same every time.  
It may look messy in the middle.  
That is okay.

This dance always brings the cube back safely.

---

### 👀 What to Look For
- The **Working Door** at the front‑right.
- A **corner fish** swimming in the door.
- The fish’s **two side colors** (not the yellow).

During the dance, many pieces may look mixed.
That is expected.

---

### 🧱 Rules for This Step
- Fish appear **only here**.
- **Only corners** are fish.
- The **net is always in your right hand**.
- Wherever your right hand is catching, that place is the **Working Door**.
- We use **Catch the Fish**.
- We do a **safe dance that always comes back**.
- We do the **same dance on purpose**.
- We do **not count**.
- We stop by **looking**, not guessing.

---
## ✋ The Moves

### Catch the Fish
This move is **the same move** you already know.

It is just like **Catch the Butterfly**,  
but now the piece is **under the water**, not in the sky.

That’s why we call it **Catch the Fish**.

How to Catch the Fish:
- The fish **swims out of the way of the net**.
- The **net drops down**.
- The fish **swims into the net**.
- The net **comes back up**.

When you Catch the Fish:
- The fish **may swim up into the net**,  
    or
- The fish **may swim back down and be let go**.

That is one **Catch the Fish**.

---
### How to use Catch the Fish
1. Hold the cube so the **Working Door stays by your right hand**.
2. Look at the **fish in the door**.
3. Catch the Fish **once**.
4. Stop and **look at the fish again**.

Each time you do this,  
the fish will **wiggle and turn**.

Sometimes it takes more than one wiggle.

That is okay.

---
### When to stop
You stop when:
- The fish is **home**, and
- All its colors **match the walls around it**.

As soon as it looks right,  
**do not wiggle it again**.

A new fish will come visit the door.  
Now help that fish.

___
### 🌟 Sometimes the Yellow Top Looks Perfect (But the Cube Does Not)
This happens sometimes.  
You did nothing wrong.

#### What you might see

You may see this:

- The **yellow top looks perfect**.  
    All the yellow pieces match each other.
- The rest of the cube looks **mixed and messy**.

This can feel strange.  
It is expected.

The cube is ready for its **last kind of help**.

---
### 🧠 What to Do

#### 1. Let the cube calm down
When the yellow top looks perfect  
but the rest looks messy:

- Keep using **Catch the Fish**
- After each catch, **stop and look**

Each time:

- The yellow top stays together
- The rest of the cube slowly **settles**

Keep going until you see this:

- Almost the **whole cube looks calm**
- Only **two corner fish** are still unhappy
    - one **yellow**
    - one **white**

They are in the right places,  
but they are **turned the wrong way**.

Now stop.

---
#### 2. Turn the whole cube once (the big turn)
Now we do something special.
- Slowly **turn the entire cube once**.

After the turn:
- The **white side** is now the **net**.
- The net is **under your right hand**.
- Wherever the net is,  
    that place is the **Working Door**.

This is the only time the **net changes color**.

That’s okay.  
Your **right hand** still tells you where the door is.

---
#### 3. Help the yellow fish
Now we use the Working Door.

A fish usually needs to **wiggle a few times**.

- Gently turn the **top**  
    until the **yellow fish is above the door**

Now:
- Catch the Fish **once**
- Stop and look

If it is **not facing the right way yet**, 
help it again.

Each time you do this,  
the yellow fish **will wiggle and turn**.

When the yellow fish faces the right way,  
**leave it alone**.

---
#### 4. Help the white fish
The white fish may also need to **wiggle a few times**.

- Gently turn the **top**
    until the **white fish is above the door**

Now:
- Catch the Fish **once**
- Stop and look

If it is **not facing the right way yet**, 
help it again.

Each time you do this,  
the yellow fish **will wiggle and turn**.

When it looks right,  
**do not catch it again**.

---
#### 5. One last straighten

Now the fish are happy.

There may be just **one thing left**:

- Gently turn the **yellow top**  
    until everything lines up

That’s it.

---
## 🔁 If Something Looks Weird (fully safe framing)

While a fish is wiggling:

- Other pieces may move.
- Colors may look mixed.

This is expected.

After each wiggle:
- The cube is still **safe**.
- The **Working Door is unchanged**.

If the fish is not done yet,  
give it another gentle wiggle.

---

## ✅ Check When You’re Done
You are done when:
- All **corners** are happy.
- All **faces match**.
- The cube looks calm and complete.

🎉 You did it.

## 🧠 Adult Notes (Optional)

### Catching as the unifying idea

Throughout the guide, the learner performs one core **conceptual action**: _catching a piece through the Working Door_. The story determines how that action is carried out.

- To **catch a butterfly**, the learner aims for a piece in the **sky**. Catching it naturally involves lifting the net.
- To **catch a fish**, the learner aims for a piece in the **water**. Catching it naturally involves dropping the net.

The learner is never taught net direction as a rule. Instead, the animal being helped determines what “catching” means. This keeps intent primary and mechanics secondary.

---
### Conceptual continuity, mechanical distinction

Although Catch the Butterfly and Catch the Fish are **mechanically different** (top‑layer vs bottom‑layer handling), they are intentionally taught as the **same story action** applied in different environments. The learner reuses:

- the same intent (“help this piece by catching it”),
- the same physical cue (the net under the right hand),
- and the same safety expectation (the cube always returns to a stable shape after each catch).

This allows the child to carry understanding forward without needing to learn new abstract rules.

---
### Repetition without counting

Each time Catch the Fish is performed, the orientation of the corner changes, even though the corner may move between top and bottom layers as part of the process. Children are never asked to count repetitions or to complete full cycles. Instead, the guide enforces a strict rhythm:

**catch once → stop → look → decide**

Releasing a corner back into the Working Door is framed as **normal progress**, not undoing work. This is essential to preventing frustration at this stage.

---
### Yellow‑top‑appears‑solved case

A common and expected situation in Step 4 is that the **entire yellow layer becomes internally consistent** (all yellow pieces correct relative to one another), while the rest of the cube appears scrambled. This is not an error.

Continued use of Catch the Fish naturally drives the cube toward a “calm” configuration where **all pieces except two corners** are solved, with those two corners correctly placed but twisted. This visually calm‑except‑for‑two state is the correct stopping point before the final cube rotation.

---
### Ceremonial rotation and Working Door reassignment

The single cube rotation in Step 4 is intentional and unique. It does not introduce a new move; it repositions the **working area** so the same catching action can be used to finish the last two corners.

After the rotation:

- the **white layer becomes the net**,
- the net remains anchored to the **child’s right hand**, and
- the **Working Door is redefined** as the place under the new net.

The Working Door is therefore not a fixed physical slot. It is a relational concept: _the place where catching happens_.

---
### Final order choice

The guide instructs solving the **yellow corner first**, followed by the **white corner**. This ordering is intentional. It minimizes cleanup by requiring only a single final alignment of the yellow face. Solving in the opposite order is mechanically valid but increases cognitive load and is avoided in the child‑facing flow.

---
### Design intent

Although Catch the Fish participates in a closed repeating structure mechanically, that structure is intentionally hidden from the learner. Describing the effect as a fish that “wiggles and turns” communicates the observable result without abstraction, counting, or blind repetition.

Step 4 prioritizes **confidence and trust** over efficiency. The learner always knows:

- what they are doing,
- why it helps,
- and when to stop.

At every point, the cube remains safe and recoverable.
