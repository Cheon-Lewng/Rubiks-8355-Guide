# Child-Friendly Rubik’s Cube Guide
## The 8355 Method (Story-First, ELI3)

---

## Purpose

This project is a **child-friendly guide to solving a 3×3 Rubik’s Cube**, designed for:

- children ages ~3–10
- absolute beginners
- readers with no cube vocabulary

The guide prioritizes:

- understanding
- confidence
- playfulness

**Not speed, notation fluency, or cube theory.**

Adults are helpers, not the audience.

---

## The 8355 Structure

The cube is solved in **four intentional phases**.
Each number represents how many pieces are solved.

1. **8 – Foundations**
   - Make the daisy
   - Make the white cross
   - Solve **three** white corners (stop at 3)

2. **3 – Middle Layer**
   - Solve **three** middle edges
   - Use the Working Door for controlled loading

3. **5 – Last Edges**
   - Solve all remaining edges
   - Complete the yellow cross
   - Learn relative (non-algorithmic) solving

4. **5 – Last Corners**
   - Solve the final five corners
   - Use intentional repetition (the Dance)

Only **one core move** is used throughout the guide.

---

## Core Teaching Principles

### 1. Story Comes First
Every action must make sense as:
- a story
- a physical action
- a clear cause → effect

No step should feel like “do this because it works.”

---

### 2. One Algorithm Rule

The entire guide is built on **one conceptual action**:

🦋 _Catch the Butterfly_ / 🐟 _Catch the Fish_

This is called “one algorithm” because the learner performs:

- the same intention (_catch this friend_),
- with the same physical cue (the **net** at the Working Door),
- and the same safety expectation (the cube always returns to structure).

The **mechanical details change with the story environment**:

- Butterflies live in the **sky**, so catching them begins by lifting the net.
- Fish live in the **water**, so catching them begins by lowering the net.

These are not taught as separate procedures.
The animal being helped determines how catching works.

From a notation perspective, the sequences are different.
From the learner’s perspective, there is only **one thing to understand**.

---

### 3. Progressive Unlocking

The cube begins restricted and becomes more expressive:
- Early steps limit moves and ideas
- Later steps introduce intuition and cycles
- Complexity is revealed, not assumed

## Language Rules (ELI3)

### ✅ Allowed in child-facing text
- short sentences
- concrete nouns
- animals, objects, characters
- physical actions
- emotions (“lost,” “safe,” “stuck”)

### 🚫 Forbidden in child-facing text
(Allowed only in adult notes)
- algorithm
- permutation
- orientation
- clockwise / counterclockwise
- adjacent

## Canonical Metaphors (Design-Locked)

These metaphors are **fixed** and must never drift.

---

### 🟨 The Sun
- The yellow center
- Always faces up
- The sun never moves (until the final ceremonial rotation)

---

### 🤍 Petals
- White edge pieces
- Used **only** during:
  - the daisy
  - the white cross
- Retired after Step 1

---

### 🕸️ The Net
- Any cube face used to perform a move
- Nets can go:
  - **up**
  - **down**
- A net is always a cube face, never a tool

---

### 🦋 Butterfly
A piece handled when it is in the **top layer**.

- Butterflies live in the sky
- Butterflies escape when the net lifts
- Butterflies are defined by **location**, not type

**Scope:**
- Used in Steps 1–3
- In Step 3:
  - butterflies may escape
  - butterflies are NOT “caught” to solve pieces

---

### 🐟 Fish (Final Step Only)

A corner handled when it sits in the **Working Door** during Step 4.

- Fish are affected when the net goes **down first**
- Fish are solved through intentional repetition (the Dance)
- Only corners are fish

Fish appear **only** in Step 4.

---

## The Working Door (Canonical Concept)

Throughout the entire solve, one location is kept open on purpose.
This is the **Working Door**.

The Working Door:
- is always the same physical location
- is intentionally unsolved
- is how pieces enter and leave safely

By step:
- Step 1 – empty on purpose
- Step 2 – loads middle edges
- Step 3 – cycles edges
- Step 4 – twists corners

The Working Door is:
✅ intentional
✅ safe
❌ not a mistake

In the final step:
- the Working Door always holds a fish
- white fish may appear but cannot be solved there
- the Working Door never moves

## Cube Orientation Rules

### Allowed Cube Rotation (Steps 1–3)

The cube may be rotated around its **vertical axis** (as if it were sitting on a table and gently spun like a top).

This type of rotation is allowed throughout **Steps 1–3** and is used to:

- change which face is in front,
- and therefore change which side serves as the **Working Door**,
    **without changing the story or the structure of the cube**.

During these rotations:

- 🟨 **Yellow must always remain on top**
- ⬜ **White must always remain on the bottom**

These rotations are observational and positional only.
They do **not** introduce new moves or new mechanics.

Any rotation that would move yellow off the top or white off the bottom is **not allowed** unless explicitly instructed.

#### Why Some Rotations Are Restricted

The guide restricts cube rotations **only** to preserve a stable story world:

- the Sun stays in the sky,
- the floor stays beneath it.

The restriction is **not** meant to freeze the cube in space.

Spinning the cube horizontally to look at different sides is expected and natural.
Tilting, flipping, or casually re‑orienting the cube vertically would break the story and is intentionally avoided.

### Final Cube Rotation (Single Exception)

The cube is rotated **exactly once** in the entire guide.

- Happens only in Step 4
- Used only to finish the last two twisted corners
- Treated as a special, celebratory moment

After this rotation:
- yellow does not need to remain on top

## Repetition Rules

### ✅ Guided Iteration (Allowed)
- repeating a routine
- visible goal
- learner knows when to stop by sight

### ✅ Closed Cycle (Final Step Only)
- repetition is intentional
- cube always returns to structure
- only corner orientation changes

### ❌ Blind Repetition (Forbidden)
- guessing
- counting “until it works”
- unexplained loops

In Step 3, butterflies may still escape into the top layer, but solving is no longer done by catching pieces.
This transition from actions to relationships is intentional.

---

## Success Criteria

This guide is successful if:
- a child can complete Step 1 with minimal help
- mistakes feel safe and fixable
- the child can explain the story to someone else
